## DCF Basics: The Valuation Logic Before The Code

Before getting into `valuator_multi_phase`, it helps to step back and explain the DCF itself in plain English. A Discounted Cash Flow analysis is one of the core methods bankers, investors, and corporate development teams use to estimate the intrinsic value of a company.

A concise way to frame it is:

> A company is worth the present value of the cash flows it can generate for its capital providers.

That sentence contains almost every major DCF concept. There are future cash flows. There is a present value. There is a discount rate that reflects risk. There are capital providers: debt and equity holders. And there is a bridge from the value of the whole operating business to the value attributable to shareholders.

### Why Future Cash Flows Need To Be Discounted

A dollar received five years from now is not worth the same as a dollar received today. First, money has time value. A dollar today can be invested and earn a return. Second, future cash flows are uncertain. Revenue may miss plan, margins may compress, competition may intensify, capital needs may increase, or macro conditions may change.

The basic present value formula is:

```text
Present Value = Future Cash Flow / (1 + Discount Rate)^n
```

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Future Cash Flow | The cash the business is expected to generate in a future year |
| Discount Rate | The required return investors demand for bearing the risk of those cash flows |
| n | The number of years into the future the cash flow is received |
| Present Value | What that future cash flow is worth today |

If the discount rate is higher, the present value is lower. That is why risk matters so much in valuation.

### Intrinsic Value Versus Market Price

DCF analysis tries to estimate intrinsic value, not simply observe the current market price.

Market price is what investors are currently paying for the stock. It can move every day based on earnings releases, sentiment, rates, liquidity, news, positioning, or short-term technical factors.

Intrinsic value is the analyst's estimate of what the business is worth based on fundamentals: cash generation, growth, margins, reinvestment needs, risk, and capital structure.

That distinction is important in M&A. A buyer is not just asking, "Where is the stock trading today?" The buyer is asking, "What is this business worth to us, under our assumptions, with our view of growth, margin, synergies, financing, and risk?"

### The Two Common DCF Approaches

Most M&A DCFs are built as unlevered DCFs. That means they value the operating business before the impact of financing decisions. The cash flow used is Free Cash Flow to the Firm, often abbreviated as FCFF or unlevered free cash flow.

An unlevered DCF answers:

> What is the value of the operating assets available to all capital providers, before deciding how the company is financed?

That produces enterprise value. Enterprise value is the value of the core operating business independent of how much debt or cash sits on the balance sheet.

The bridge is:

```text
Enterprise Value
+ Cash and Non-Operating Assets
- Debt
- Preferred Stock
- Minority Interest
= Equity Value

Equity Value / Diluted Shares Outstanding = Implied Share Price
```

There is also a levered DCF, which values cash flow available only to equity holders after interest expense, debt repayment, and debt issuance. That approach can be useful in some financial sponsor or dividend-discount contexts, but the unlevered approach is usually cleaner for M&A because it separates operating value from financing structure.

The key rule is consistency: unlevered free cash flow should be discounted at WACC, while levered free cash flow should be discounted at the cost of equity.

## The Standard DCF Workflow

A practical DCF usually follows nine steps.

### 1. Choose The Valuation Date, Currency, And Perimeter

The valuation date determines what market data, balance sheet figures, share count, and filing information are current. The currency matters because discount rates, inflation, and risk-free rates should match the currency of the cash flows. The perimeter defines what is being valued: the entire company, a division, a carve-out, a geography, or a standalone asset.

In M&A, this is also where the team decides whether the case is a standalone valuation, a synergy case, a management case, a downside case, or a sponsor case.

### 2. Gather Historical Financials

The model starts from historical operating data. At minimum, the analyst needs revenue, operating income or EBIT, taxes, depreciation and amortization, capital expenditures, working capital, cash, debt, and diluted shares outstanding.

Historical data is not just a starting point. It is a reality check. If a company has never produced margins above 12%, a terminal margin of 25% requires a strong explanation. If revenue growth has slowed from 20% to 5%, the forecast should explain why growth reaccelerates.

### 3. Forecast Revenue

Revenue is normally the first operating forecast because many other line items are driven as a percentage of revenue.

| Method | Example |
|---|---|
| Top-down market sizing | Market size x market share |
| Volume x price | Units sold x average selling price |
| Segment build | Product A + Product B + Product C |
| Store or unit growth | Number of locations x revenue per location |
| Simple growth rates | Prior-year revenue x (1 + growth rate) |

A good DCF does not treat revenue growth as a random percentage. It ties growth to a business story: market expansion, pricing power, unit growth, customer retention, new products, geographic expansion, or synergies.

### 4. Forecast Margins And Operating Profit

After revenue, the model forecasts profitability. The key operating profit measure in most unlevered DCFs is EBIT, or Earnings Before Interest and Taxes.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Gross Margin | Revenue minus cost of goods sold, divided by revenue |
| EBITDA | Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization |
| D&A | A non-cash expense reflecting use of assets and intangibles |
| EBIT | Operating income after D&A, before interest and taxes |
| Operating Margin | EBIT divided by revenue |

EBIT is used because it measures operating profit before capital structure. Interest expense is excluded because the unlevered DCF values the business before financing.

### 5. Calculate Taxes And NOPAT

The next step is to convert EBIT into after-tax operating profit. This is called NOPAT, or Net Operating Profit After Tax.

```text
NOPAT = EBIT x (1 - Tax Rate)
```

NOPAT represents the profit generated by the operating business after taxes, but before financing costs. It is not the same as net income because it excludes interest expense and other financing effects.

### 6. Convert Accounting Profit Into Free Cash Flow

DCF does not value accounting earnings. It values cash flow.

The most common unlevered free cash flow formula is:

```text
Unlevered Free Cash Flow = EBIT x (1 - Tax Rate)
                           + Depreciation and Amortization
                           - Capital Expenditures
                           - Increase in Net Working Capital
```

A simpler version of the same idea is used in the Python model:

```text
FCFF = NOPAT - Reinvestment
```

The business creates after-tax operating profit, but not all of it is distributable. Some of it must be reinvested to support growth.

### 7. Estimate The Discount Rate

For an unlevered DCF, the discount rate is usually WACC, or Weighted Average Cost of Capital.

```text
WACC = Cost of Equity x Equity Weight
     + After-Tax Cost of Debt x Debt Weight
```

The cost of equity is often estimated using CAPM:

```text
Cost of Equity = Risk-Free Rate + Beta x Equity Risk Premium
```

The cost of debt is the company's borrowing cost. Because interest is generally tax deductible, DCF models use the after-tax cost of debt:

```text
After-Tax Cost of Debt = Pre-Tax Cost of Debt x (1 - Tax Rate)
```

### 8. Discount The Explicit Forecast Cash Flows

Most DCF models forecast a finite period, often five to ten years. This is called the explicit forecast period.

Each year's free cash flow is discounted back to the valuation date:

```text
PV of Year 1 FCF = Year 1 FCF / (1 + WACC)^1
PV of Year 2 FCF = Year 2 FCF / (1 + WACC)^2
PV of Year 3 FCF = Year 3 FCF / (1 + WACC)^3
```

Some models use a mid-year convention, which assumes cash flows are received throughout the year rather than all on the final day of the year.

### 9. Calculate Terminal Value

A company does not stop operating at the end of the explicit forecast period. Terminal value captures the value of all cash flows after the final forecast year.

Perpetual growth method:

```text
Terminal Value = Final Year FCF x (1 + Terminal Growth Rate)
                 / (WACC - Terminal Growth Rate)
```

Exit multiple method:

```text
Terminal Value = Terminal Year Metric x Exit Multiple
```

For example:

```text
Terminal Value = Terminal Year EBITDA x Exit EV / EBITDA Multiple
```

Terminal value often represents a large percentage of total enterprise value. That is not automatically wrong, but it means terminal assumptions require serious review.

## The Terms Analysts Debate Most

DCF models can look precise, but the valuation is only as good as the assumptions.

| Assumption | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Revenue growth | Drives the size of the business in future years |
| Operating margin | Determines how much revenue converts into operating profit |
| Reinvestment | Determines how much profit must be put back into the business |
| WACC | Converts future cash flows into present value |
| Terminal growth | Drives cash flows beyond the explicit forecast |
| Terminal margin | Tests whether the terminal year is economically realistic |
| Exit multiple | Imports market valuation assumptions into terminal value |
| Net debt | Bridges enterprise value to equity value |
| Share count | Converts total equity value into per-share value |

A useful way to review a DCF is to ask whether the assumptions tell one coherent story. A high-growth company usually needs high reinvestment. A mature company usually has slower growth, more stable margins, and a lower risk profile.

## Common DCF Mistakes

The most common DCF errors are conceptual, not mathematical.

| Mistake | Why it is a problem |
|---|---|
| Discounting net income at WACC | Net income is after interest and belongs to equity holders |
| Forgetting reinvestment | Growth requires capital; ignoring reinvestment overstates free cash flow |
| Using an aggressive terminal growth rate | No company can outgrow the economy forever |
| Using an unnormalized terminal year | Terminal value should be based on a steady-state year |
| Double-counting cash or debt | Cash and debt belong in the enterprise-to-equity bridge |
| Treating DCF as a point estimate | A DCF should usually be presented as a range |
| Letting the model invent assumptions | In M&A, assumptions need ownership, approval, and documentation |

## How This Primer Maps To The Python Valuation Engine

| DCF concept | How the model captures it |
|---|---|
| Starting revenue | `revenue_base` |

| Operating profitability | `current_operating_margin` converging to `terminal_operating_margin` |
| Taxes | `current_effective_tax_rate` converging to `marginal_tax_rate` |
| Reinvestment | Sales-to-capital ratio and projected change in revenue |
| Business risk | `unlevered_beta` converging to `terminal_unlevered_beta` |

| Enterprise-to-equity bridge | Cash and non-operating assets added; debt subtracted |

This is why the agent design matters. The DCF engine can perform the calculations consistently, but the judgment around growth, margins, reinvestment, WACC, terminal assumptions, and synergies should remain human-owned.

## Inside `valuator_multi_phase`: Investment Principles And The Anatomy Of A DCF

Before wrapping this function in an agent, it is worth understanding what it actually does and why its parameter design reflects valuation theory rather than just spreadsheet convention.

The foundational principle is that every asset is worth the present value of the cash flows it is expected to generate over its lifetime, discounted at a rate that reflects the risk of those cash flows.

That means valuation comes down to three questions:

1. How much cash will this business generate?
2. How long can it generate excess returns?
3. What rate of return does an investor require to own this business?

The function translates all three into a year-by-year free cash flow projection, discounts those flows back to today, adds terminal value for everything beyond the explicit forecast period, and adjusts for cash, debt, and non-operating assets to get from firm value to equity value.

### The Three-Cycle Growth Architecture

The model does not ask for a single growth rate or a flat forecast period. It uses three sequential cycles, each with its own start rate, end rate, length, and convergence speed.

| Parameter | What it controls |
|---|---|
| `revenue_growth_rate_cycle1_begin` | Growth rate at the start of the high-growth phase |
| `revenue_growth_rate_cycle1_end` | Growth rate at the end of cycle 1 |
| `revenue_convergance_periods_cycle1` | How quickly growth decelerates within cycle 1 |
| `length_of_cylcle1` | How many years cycle 1 lasts |
| `revenue_growth_rate_cycle2_begin` | Growth rate entering the transition phase |
| `revenue_growth_rate_cycle2_end` | Growth rate exiting the transition phase |
| `revenue_convergance_periods_cycle2` | Deceleration speed within cycle 2 |
| `length_of_cylcle2` | Duration of cycle 2 |
| `revenue_growth_rate_cycle3_begin` | Growth rate entering the mature phase |
| `revenue_growth_rate_cycle3_end` | Terminal growth rate |
| `revenue_convergance_periods_cycle3` | Deceleration speed within cycle 3 |
| `length_of_cylcle3` | Duration of the explicit mature phase before terminal value |

The terminal growth rate is cycle 3's ending rate. It should not exceed the risk-free rate in the governed workflow.

### Cost Of Capital Parameters

The WACC is not a single number that stays fixed over the forecast. This model lets the cost of capital evolve over time, which is appropriate for businesses that are expected to change risk profile or capital structure as they mature.

| Parameter | What it represents |
|---|---|
| `risk_free_rate` | Baseline return on a zero-risk investment in the same currency |
| `ERP` | Additional return investors demand for equities over risk-free assets |
| `unlevered_beta` | Business operating risk stripped of financial leverage |
| `terminal_unlevered_beta` | Where beta converges by the end of the forecast |
| `year_beta_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_beta` | When beta starts moving toward terminal beta |
| `current_pretax_cost_of_debt` | Company's current pre-tax borrowing cost |
| `terminal_pretax_cost_of_debt` | Cost of debt at maturity |
| `year_cost_of_debt_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_cost_of_debt` | When cost of debt convergence begins |
| `equity_value` | Current market capitalization or estimate |
| `debt_value` | Market or book value of interest-bearing debt |

The levered beta used in CAPM is derived internally from unlevered beta and the debt-to-equity ratio each year.

### Tax, Margin, Reinvestment, And Balance Sheet Parameters

The tax path starts from the current effective tax rate and converges toward the marginal tax rate. Operating margin starts from the current operating margin and moves toward a terminal operating margin. Reinvestment is tied to projected revenue growth through the sales-to-capital ratio.

```text
Reinvestment = Change in Revenue / Sales-to-Capital Ratio
```

That linkage is important. You cannot project high revenue growth without accounting for the reinvestment required to support it.

Balance sheet and non-operating parameters include `revenue_base`, `cash_and_non_operating_asset`, `current_invested_capital`, and `asset_liquidation_during_negative_growth`.

The internal computation flows as:

```text
Risk-Free Rate + Beta x ERP  ->  Cost of Equity
Cost of Debt x (1 - Tax Rate)  ->  After-Tax Cost of Debt
WACC = Weighted average of Cost of Equity and After-Tax Cost of Debt

Revenue x Operating Margin  ->  EBIT
EBIT x (1 - Tax Rate)  ->  NOPAT
NOPAT - Reinvestment  ->  FCFF

Sum of discounted FCFFs + Discounted Terminal Value  ->  Value of Operating Assets
Value of Operating Assets + Cash and Non-Operating Assets  ->  Firm Value
Firm Value - Debt  ->  Intrinsic Equity Value
```

The convergence architecture is the model's real sophistication. Rather than asking for a single assumption, it asks: where are you today, where are you going, and how long does the transition take?

## The Key Design Principle

The central principle is simple:

> Use deterministic code for valuation math. Use the agent for orchestration, data gathering, questioning, and explanation.

A DCF has two very different categories of inputs, and treating them the same is where many demos go wrong.

Historical facts such as revenue, operating income, tax expense, cash, debt, shares outstanding, and filing dates can be retrieved and normalized from public filings. These values have a source, a date, and a chain of custody.

Forward-looking assumptions such as future revenue growth, target operating margin, terminal growth, beta, equity risk premium, synergy capture, integration costs, and control premium require human judgment. They reflect a deal narrative: the management case, the banker's base case, the sponsor's downside, or the strategic buyer's synergy view.

A good M&A valuation agent must know the difference. It should retrieve the first category and ask for the second. The deterministic Python engine sits at the bottom, and the two are connected by a controlled handoff through human assumption capture.

## Where Google ADK Fits

Google ADK lets developers define agents with tools built from custom Python functions. A Python function added to an agent's tools list is automatically wrapped as a FunctionTool. The framework inspects the function signature, docstring, type hints, and defaults to generate the schema the model uses to call it.

That is the right pattern for a DCF function. ADK 2.0 also supports graph workflows that can pause and request human input through `RequestInput`, which is valuable here because some assumptions must be explicitly provided or approved by a banker, not inferred.

One important caveat: ADK 2.0 is currently marked Beta, and Google notes it may introduce breaking changes. For production deployments at a bank, treat it as an evaluation candidate and route it through normal platform engineering, model risk, security, and change management processes.

## Step 1: Wrap The DCF Function As A Tool

The notebook function should not be exposed to the agent directly. Instead, create a wrapper that validates inputs, checks for missing fields, catches obvious inconsistencies, calls `valuator_multi_phase()`, and returns a JSON-friendly result.

```python
# valuation/dcf_tool.py

from typing import Any, Dict
from valuation.dcf_model import valuator_multi_phase

REQUIRED_DCF_KEYS = [
    "risk_free_rate", "ERP", "equity_value", "debt_value",
    "unlevered_beta", "terminal_unlevered_beta",
    "year_beta_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_beta",
    "current_pretax_cost_of_debt", "terminal_pretax_cost_of_debt",
    "year_cost_of_debt_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_cost_of_debt",
    "current_effective_tax_rate", "marginal_tax_rate",
    "year_effective_tax_rate_begin_to_converge_marginal_tax_rate",
    "revenue_base",
    "revenue_growth_rate_cycle1_begin", "revenue_growth_rate_cycle1_end",
    "revenue_growth_rate_cycle2_begin", "revenue_growth_rate_cycle2_end",
    "revenue_growth_rate_cycle3_begin", "revenue_growth_rate_cycle3_end",
    "revenue_convergance_periods_cycle1",
    "revenue_convergance_periods_cycle2",
    "revenue_convergance_periods_cycle3",
    "length_of_cylcle1", "length_of_cylcle2", "length_of_cylcle3",
    "current_sales_to_capital_ratio", "terminal_sales_to_capital_ratio",
    "year_sales_to_capital_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_sales_to_capital",
    "current_operating_margin", "terminal_operating_margin",
    "year_operating_margin_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_operating_margin",
]


def run_dcf_valuation(params: Dict[str, Any]) -> Dict[str, Any]:
    """
    Runs a multi-phase discounted cash flow valuation.

    Percentage values should be expressed as decimals. For example, 4% is 0.04.
    Call only after historical facts and forward-looking assumptions have been
    collected, validated, and logged.
    """
    missing = [key for key in REQUIRED_DCF_KEYS if key not in params]
    if missing:
        return {
            "status": "missing_inputs",
            "missing_inputs": missing,
            "message": "DCF cannot run until all required inputs are provided.",
        }

    terminal_growth = params["revenue_growth_rate_cycle3_end"]
    risk_free_rate = params["risk_free_rate"]

    if terminal_growth > risk_free_rate:
        return {
            "status": "invalid_inputs",
            "message": (
                "Terminal growth exceeds the risk-free rate. "
                "Please revise or explicitly approve this assumption."
            ),
            "terminal_growth_rate": terminal_growth,
            "risk_free_rate": risk_free_rate,
        }

    result = valuator_multi_phase(**params)
    valuation_table = result["valuation"].reset_index().to_dict(orient="records")

    return {
        "status": "success",
        "firm_value": float(result["firm_value"]),
        "intrinsic_equity_value": float(result["equity_value"]),
        "value_of_operating_assets": float(result["value_of_operating_assets"]),
        "cash_and_non_operating_asset": float(result["cash_and_non_operating_asset"]),
        "debt_value": float(result["debt_value"]),
        "valuation_table": valuation_table,
    }
```

In ADK, a simple agent wiring could look like this:

## Step 2: Build SEC/EDGAR Tools

The SEC provides JSON-based APIs through `data.sec.gov` for company submissions and XBRL financial statement data. These APIs require no authentication or API keys and cover forms including 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, 20-F, and 40-F.

Useful endpoints:

```text
https://www.sec.gov/files/company_tickers.json
https://data.sec.gov/submissions/CIK##########.json
https://data.sec.gov/api/xbrl/companyfacts/CIK##########.json
https://data.sec.gov/api/xbrl/companyconcept/CIK##########/us-gaap/<TAG>.json
```

```python
# data/sec_tools.py

from typing import Any, Dict
import requests

SEC_HEADERS = {
    "User-Agent": "Your Firm Name valuation-platform@example.com",
    "Accept-Encoding": "gzip, deflate",
}


def _sec_get_json(url: str) -> Dict[str, Any]:
    response = requests.get(url, headers=SEC_HEADERS, timeout=30)
    response.raise_for_status()
    return response.json()


def _format_cik(cik: int | str) -> str:
    return f"CIK{int(cik):010d}"


def lookup_company(query: str) -> Dict[str, Any]:
    """Resolves a ticker or company name to SEC company metadata."""
    url = "https://www.sec.gov/files/company_tickers.json"
    data = _sec_get_json(url)
    q = query.strip().upper()
    matches = []

    for item in data.values():
        ticker = item["ticker"].upper()
        title = item["title"].upper()
        if q == ticker or q in title:
            matches.append({
                "ticker": item["ticker"],
                "title": item["title"],
                "cik": item["cik_str"],
                "cik_padded": _format_cik(item["cik_str"]),
            })

    if not matches:
        return {
            "status": "not_found",
            "query": query,
            "message": "No SEC company match found.",
        }

    return {
        "status": "success",
        "query": query,
        "best_match": matches[0],
        "matches": matches[:10],
    }


def fetch_company_facts(cik: int | str) -> Dict[str, Any]:
    """Fetches SEC XBRL company facts."""
    cik_padded = _format_cik(cik)
    url = f"https://data.sec.gov/api/xbrl/companyfacts/{cik_padded}.json"
    return _sec_get_json(url)
```

One operational caveat worth noting: the SEC's fair-access guidelines currently limit users to no more than 10 requests per second. Production architecture needs rate limiting, retries with backoff, caching, and request logging built in from the start.

## Step 3: Normalize XBRL Facts Into A DCF Starting Point

SEC XBRL data is powerful but not a ready-made DCF input sheet. Different companies use different tags for similar concepts. Some are annual; others quarterly. Some are point-in-time; others duration-based. A normalization layer resolves this.

```python
# data/sec_normalizer.py

from typing import Any, Dict, Iterable, Optional


def _latest_annual_usd_fact(
    company_facts: Dict[str, Any],
    tag_candidates: Iterable[str],
) -> Optional[float]:
    us_gaap = company_facts.get("facts", {}).get("us-gaap", {})

    for tag in tag_candidates:
        concept = us_gaap.get(tag)
        if not concept:
            continue

        usd_facts = concept.get("units", {}).get("USD", [])
        annual = [
            item for item in usd_facts
            if item.get("form") == "10-K" and item.get("fp") == "FY"
        ]
        if not annual:
            continue

        annual = sorted(
            annual,
            key=lambda x: (x.get("fy", 0), x.get("filed", "")),
            reverse=True,
        )
        return float(annual[0]["val"])

    return None


def build_financial_snapshot(company_facts: Dict[str, Any]) -> Dict[str, Any]:
    """
    Converts SEC company facts into a compact DCF starting point.

    Extracts historical facts only. Does not generate future assumptions.
    """
    revenue = _latest_annual_usd_fact(
        company_facts,
        ["RevenueFromContractWithCustomerExcludingAssessedTax",
         "Revenues", "SalesRevenueNet"],
    )
    operating_income = _latest_annual_usd_fact(
        company_facts, ["OperatingIncomeLoss"])
    tax_expense = _latest_annual_usd_fact(
        company_facts, ["IncomeTaxExpenseBenefit"])
    pretax_income = _latest_annual_usd_fact(
        company_facts,
        ["IncomeLossFromContinuingOperationsBeforeIncomeTaxesExtraordinaryItemsNoncontrollingInterest",
         "IncomeLossFromContinuingOperationsBeforeIncomeTaxes"],
    )
    cash = _latest_annual_usd_fact(
        company_facts,
        ["CashAndCashEquivalentsAtCarryingValue",
         "CashCashEquivalentsRestrictedCashAndRestrictedCashEquivalents"],
    )
    debt = _latest_annual_usd_fact(
        company_facts,
        ["LongTermDebtAndFinanceLeaseObligations",
         "LongTermDebtNoncurrent", "LongTermDebt"],
    )

    operating_margin = (
        operating_income / revenue
        if revenue and operating_income is not None else None
    )
    effective_tax_rate = (
        tax_expense / pretax_income
        if pretax_income and pretax_income > 0 and tax_expense is not None else None
    )

    return {
        "status": "success",
        "revenue_base": revenue,
        "operating_income": operating_income,
        "current_operating_margin": operating_margin,
        "current_effective_tax_rate": effective_tax_rate,
        "cash_and_non_operating_asset": cash,
        "debt_value_book": debt,
        "notes": [
            "These are historical starting points from public filings.",
            "Future growth, target margin, WACC, reinvestment, and terminal "
            "assumptions require human judgment.",
        ],
    }
```

This function is intentionally conservative. It extracts what is knowable from public filings and stops there. Everything forward-looking is left for the next step.

## Step 4: Ask The Human For Forward-Looking Assumptions

This is where human-in-the-loop design matters most.

An agent can retrieve historical revenue from a 10-K. It should not silently decide that a company will grow at 12% for five years. In M&A, forward-looking assumptions are part of the deal narrative. They may reflect the management case, the banker's base case, the sponsor's downside, the strategic buyer's synergy view, or street consensus. Each has a different owner and a different governance implication.

The agent should surface what it has, flag what it needs, and ask the banker to supply the rest.

**Figure 3: Human assumption capture decision flow**

```mermaid
flowchart TD
    A[Agent has historical snapshot] --> B{Are required DCF inputs complete?}
    B -->|Yes| C[Run DCF Tool]
    B -->|No| D[Generate Assumption Request]
    D --> E[Banker / Analyst Provides Inputs]
    E --> F[Validate Inputs]
    F --> G{Inputs plausible?}
    G -->|Yes| C
    G -->|No| H[Ask for revision or approval]
    H --> E
    C --> I[Produce Valuation Result]
    I --> J[Store Sources, Assumptions, and Output]
```

```python
from pydantic import BaseModel
from google.adk.events import RequestInput


class DCFUserAssumptions(BaseModel):
    risk_free_rate: float
    ERP: float
    unlevered_beta: float
    terminal_unlevered_beta: float
    current_pretax_cost_of_debt: float
    terminal_pretax_cost_of_debt: float
    marginal_tax_rate: float
    revenue_growth_rate_cycle1_begin: float
    revenue_growth_rate_cycle1_end: float
    length_of_cylcle1: int
    revenue_growth_rate_cycle2_begin: float
    revenue_growth_rate_cycle2_end: float
    length_of_cylcle2: int
    revenue_growth_rate_cycle3_begin: float
    revenue_growth_rate_cycle3_end: float
    length_of_cylcle3: int
    current_sales_to_capital_ratio: float
    terminal_sales_to_capital_ratio: float
    terminal_operating_margin: float
    additional_return_on_cost_of_capital_in_perpetuity: float


def request_dcf_assumptions(snapshot: dict):
    message = f"""
I found the following historical starting point from SEC filings:

Company: {snapshot.get("company_name")}
Ticker: {snapshot.get("ticker")}
Revenue base: {snapshot.get("revenue_base")}
Current operating margin: {snapshot.get("current_operating_margin")}
Current effective tax rate: {snapshot.get("current_effective_tax_rate")}
Cash: {snapshot.get("cash_and_non_operating_asset")}
Book debt: {snapshot.get("debt_value_book")}

I still need forward-looking assumptions to run the DCF:

- Risk-free rate and equity risk premium
- Unlevered beta (current and terminal)
- Pre-tax cost of debt (current and terminal)
- Marginal tax rate
- Three-phase revenue growth (start rate, end rate, years per phase)
- Sales-to-capital ratio (current and terminal)
- Terminal operating margin
- Long-run excess return on capital, if any

Use decimals, for example 4% as 0.04.
"""
    yield RequestInput(
        message=message,
        payload=snapshot,
        response_schema=DCFUserAssumptions,
    )
```

In production, this free-text interaction should be replaced with a structured UI: numeric input fields, dropdown scenario labels, validation ranges, approval workflow, assumption comments, and lineage tags. The goal is not just to collect the numbers, but to record who provided them and in what context.

| Input | Source | Owner | Required control |
|---|---|---|---|
| Historical revenue | SEC / data room | System | Source reference |
| Current operating margin | SEC / normalized | System | Data quality flag |
| Forecast growth | Banker / management case | Human | Required approval |
| Terminal growth | Banker / valuation policy | Human | Range validation |
| WACC | Market data + assumption | Human/system | Methodology disclosure |
| Synergies | Deal team | Human | Scenario label |
| Control premium | Banker | Human | Separate from standalone DCF |

    snapshot["ticker"] = node_input["company"]["ticker"]
    snapshot["cik"] = node_input["company"]["cik"]

def merge_inputs_node(node_input: dict) -> dict:
    snapshot = node_input["snapshot"]
    assumptions = node_input["assumptions"]

    params = {}
    params["revenue_base"] = snapshot["revenue_base"]
    params["current_operating_margin"] = snapshot["current_operating_margin"]
    params["current_effective_tax_rate"] = snapshot["current_effective_tax_rate"]
    params["cash_and_non_operating_asset"] = snapshot["cash_and_non_operating_asset"]
    params["debt_value"] = snapshot["debt_value_book"]

    params.update(assumptions)

    params["year_beta_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_beta"] = 1
    params["year_cost_of_debt_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_cost_of_debt"] = 3
    params["year_effective_tax_rate_begin_to_converge_marginal_tax_rate"] = 1
    params["revenue_convergance_periods_cycle1"] = 1
    params["revenue_convergance_periods_cycle2"] = 1
    params["revenue_convergance_periods_cycle3"] = 1
    params["year_sales_to_capital_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_sales_to_capital"] = 1
    params["year_operating_margin_begins_to_converge_to_terminal_operating_margin"] = 1

    return params


root_agent = Workflow(

    edges=[
        (

## Adding Monte Carlo Simulation

The original notebook includes a Monte Carlo wrapper, and earlier work in this space demonstrates the basic mechanics well.

The enterprise version goes further. Investment banking teams rarely care about a single-point estimate. They care about ranges, and they want those ranges to reflect real business uncertainty, not arbitrary random noise.

In an M&A context, the right approach is to let the banker specify distributions. The agent should not invent them.

| Variable | Base case | Distribution |
|---|---:|---|
| Terminal margin | 18.0% | Normal, mean 18%, standard deviation 1.5% |
| Terminal growth | 3.0% | Triangular, low 2%, mode 3%, high 4% |
| Sales-to-capital | 1.3x | Normal, mean 1.3x, standard deviation 0.2x |
| ERP | 4.8% | Fixed or scenario-based |
| Revenue CAGR | 8.0% | Scenario distribution |

The Monte Carlo agent can then produce a valuation percentile range, probability of exceeding the current market price, downside/base/upside cases, key value driver sensitivities, and distribution charts.

The control requirement is the same as for the base case: the user approves the distributions. The model does not invent them.

## Production Controls For Banking Environments

Before any of this goes into a production environment, technical teams should treat it as a controlled workflow, not a productivity feature.

### Entitlement And Data Classification

The agent must know whether it is working with public data, licensed market data, internal research, confidential client data, data room content, or MNPI. Different data classes require different permissions, storage rules, and output restrictions.

### Source Lineage

Every historical number should carry structured metadata:

```json
{
  "value": 123456789,
  "metric": "Revenue",
  "source": "SEC XBRL",
  "form": "10-K",
  "filing_date": "YYYY-MM-DD",
  "accession_number": "...",
  "xbrl_tag": "RevenueFromContractWithCustomerExcludingAssessedTax"
}
```

### Assumption Ownership

Every forward-looking input should carry:

```json
{
  "assumption": "terminal_operating_margin",
  "value": 0.18,
  "scenario": "base_case",
  "provided_by": "analyst@example.com",
  "approved_by": "vp@example.com",
  "timestamp": "2026-05-11T10:30:00Z",
  "comment": "Based on management case and peer margin benchmarking."
}
```

### Model Versioning

Output should record the DCF model version, Python package version, notebook lineage, valuation date, scenario name, run ID, and input hash. This is what makes old valuations reproducible during review.

### Guardrails

The agent should refuse or escalate when required assumptions are missing, terminal growth exceeds a policy threshold, source data is stale, filings cannot be reconciled, the user lacks entitlement, or confidential data would be exposed.

## Recommended Agent Instruction

The system instruction shapes behavior more than almost anything else:

```text
You are an M&A valuation workflow assistant.

Your job is to coordinate valuation workflows, not to independently make
investment recommendations.

Always separate:
1. Historical facts from filings or approved data sources
2. Market data from approved providers
3. User-provided assumptions
4. Model-generated outputs

Never invent forward-looking assumptions. If you create an illustrative
scenario, label it clearly as illustrative and not banker-approved.

Before calling the DCF valuation tool:
- confirm all required inputs are present
- validate percentage values are expressed as decimals
- check terminal growth against policy limits
- confirm the scenario name and valuation date
- capture assumption provenance

After running the model:
- explain the key assumptions and largest value drivers
- disclose data sources
- state limitations clearly
- do not provide buy/sell/hold recommendations
```

## What Not To Automate

Some parts of M&A valuation should remain human-led regardless of what the technology can do:

- Final valuation conclusions
- Fairness opinion judgments
- Board recommendation language
- Synergy credibility assessments
- Buyer intent
- Negotiation strategy
- Legal and compliance sign-off

Agents accelerate analysis. They should not own judgment.

The right model is:

> Machine prepares, human decides, system records.

## Conclusion

The most important lesson from turning a DCF notebook into an agent is a design constraint, not a technology choice:

> The LLM should not be the valuation model.

The valuation model should be deterministic, tested, and versioned. The LLM should orchestrate company lookup, filing retrieval, financial normalization, assumption collection, validation, tool execution, explanation, and audit logging.

A demo that asks an LLM to "value this company" is interesting. A governed agentic valuation pipeline is useful.

The future of M&A technology is not replacing deal teams with agents. It is giving them better infrastructure: tools that reduce manual effort, preserve judgment, improve traceability, and make valuation work easier to review.

That is the real opportunity.
